Out of all reported hate crimes in New York State, 56% occurred in the Five Boroughs of New York City since 2010, a concerning statistic for residents and lawmakers in the area.

Elisa Flynn
December 11, 2023

New York City, known for its rich diversity, has long prided itself on being a welcoming and inclusive metropolis. However, it is home to about 42% of the New York State, so the fact that 56% of New York hate crimes occurred in the Five Boroughs should be a call to action. The concentration in hate crimes is a harsh reminder of the work that still needs to be done to preserve NYC's image of diversity and acceptance. Predominantly targeted at Jewish and Black people, these crimes range from acts of vandlaism to physical assaults and verbal abuse, often occurring to similar groups of people.

A former officer for the New York Police Department was mildly surprised by this information, despite their experience.

“The diversity of thought and culture in New York City is its greatest strength and weakness. On top of the deplorable poverty situation, hate is a theme in many neighborhoods. Gangs, white nationalists, and other groups are known for targeting specific groups of people. But almost 60% of all hate crimes in the state? I didn’t expect that.”

Data from the New York City Police Department indicated that anti-Semitic hate crimes rose 125% percent in November of 2022, and that Black people remain the most consistently targeted group overall. It’s a reality many NYC residents have to face, establishing an ever present awareness that criminal hatred is somewhat expected.

According to the NYPD, hate crimes jumped 124% year-over-year in October since the resurgence of the Israel-Gaza War earlier that month. 69 of the 101 recorded hate crimes were anti-Jewish incidents, a nearly 20% increase since last October.

According to ABC Eyewitness News, “‘We are seeing a lot of people getting slapped, getting pushed,’ said NYPD Chief of Detectives Joseph Kenny. ‘The majority of the 30 crimes that we saw that were anti-Jewish, they stemmed from graffiti, criminal mischief, aggravated harassment. Basically almost bordering on free speech.'”

There were only 30 recorded anti-Jewish crimes at the time this interview took place.

There were eight reported anti-Muslim incidents, up from zero last October, along with seven incidents classified as "anti-ethnic", up from three last year. At least four of those “anti-ethnic” incidents were anti-Palestinian incidents. According to the NYPD, a 16-year-old girl was riding a 5 train on Oct. 18 when a man pulled off her hijab while yelling at her.

Mayor Eric Adams posted on X, formerly known as Twitter, about the incident, saying that “this is your city and we will keep you safe.”

Amidst this recent spike, it is important to note the differences in crime reporting from cities to more suburban or rural areas. Many people in less populated areas also have less resources to report crimes. What is more, amidst an internationally involved conflict such as the Israel-Gaza War, all types of crimes tend to be reported more, with police usually on the lookout for public upset.

Socioeconomic disparities are emerging as a pivotal driver, with marginalized communities grappling with a disproportionate burden of economic challenges. Limited access to quality education, job opportunities, and affordable housing creates an environment ripe for frustration and resentment, setting the stage for acts of intolerance.

The polarized state of public discourse is also playing a significant role, as divisive rhetoric from political figures and controversial policies contribute to heightened social tensions. This atmosphere not only emboldens hate groups but also fosters an environment where individuals feel empowered to act on their prejudices.

The influence of social media cannot be overlooked, as online platforms serve as both a connector and catalyst for extremist ideologies. The rapid dissemination of hateful messages and the ease with which hate groups organize online are massive contributers to the alarming spike in real-world incidents.

Critics point to systemic issues within law enforcement, including racial profiling and a lack of cultural competency training, as hindrances to effectively addressing hate crimes. Establishing trust between law enforcement and communities is identified as a crucial step in dismantling systemic biases.

State lawmakers are also taking action, working on legislation to stiffen penalties for individuals found guilty of hate crimes. Governor Kathy Hochul passed legislation in late 2022 to require those convicted of hate crimes to undergo hate prevention training and counseling. This legislative push is a direct response to the wave of intolerance, designed to send a strong message that New York City will not tolerate such actions.

As the city grapples with the resurgence of intolerance, it stands firm in its commitment to promoting a culture of acceptance and understanding, ensuring that all residents can live without fear of discrimination or violence.
